# Onboarding Is a Retention Problem in Disguise

*(Medium, published 2026-01-20 — the demo persona's anchor essay; produced PIECE-0001.)*

We build onboarding to get people in and retention to keep them, and we staff, fund,
and measure the two as if they were different problems. They aren't. They're one
journey seen at two moments, and the seam between them is where good products quietly
lose people.

## The checklist that lies

A first-run checklist reliably lifts activation. It also, just as reliably, teaches
users that the product will carry them. When the scaffolding comes down in week five,
the work becomes theirs, and the habit you thought you'd built was really a habit of
being helped.

## What to measure instead

The useful question isn't "did they activate" but "did activation predict that they'd
still be here." An activation metric that doesn't correlate with retention isn't a
leading indicator. It's a vanity number with good timing.

## Designing the seam

Treat the handoff from guided to unguided as a designed moment: fade the scaffolding
on purpose, make the first unassisted win reachable, and instrument the drop where
help disappears. The teams that do this stop celebrating a number that was quietly
working against them.
